| Driver | Explanation |

4. **Prohibited practices** – e.g., “social scoring” systems, subliminal manipulation, and “real‑time remote biometric identification in public spaces” (except for narrow exceptions).  
5. **Governance architecture** – the **European Artificial Intelligence Board (EAIB)** and national supervisory authorities form a **coordinated oversight network**.  
6. **Alignment with existing EU law** – GDPR, the Product Liability Directive, and the forthcoming **AI‑Regulation in the Digital Services Act** (articles on “very large online platforms”) are cross‑referenced to avoid duplication.

**REPORT: EU AI Act – Controversies and Criticisms**

**Overview of the EU AI Act**  
The European Union’s Artificial Intelligence Act (AI Act) is the world’s first comprehensive legal framework for AI. It adopts a risk-based approach, categorizing AI systems into unacceptable risk (banned), high risk (subject to strict requirements), limited risk (transparency obligations), and minimal risk (no obligations). The Act was formally adopted in March 2024, with phased implementation starting in 2025.

**Key Controversies and Criticisms**

1. **Definition of AI – Too Broad or Too Narrow?**  
   - Critics argue the definition is overly broad, potentially covering simple algorithms like spam filters or linear regression, leading to unnecessary compliance burdens.  
   - Conversely, some experts say the definition is too narrow and may miss future AI developments, especially general-purpose AI (GPAI) systems that are not explicitly listed.  
   - *Source:* The AI Act’s definition was heavily debated during negotiations; the final text uses a list-based approach that some consider already outdated.

2. **Risk-Based Approach – A False Sense of Security?**  
   - Many civil society organizations (e.g., EDRi, Access Now) argue that the risk categories are too permissive. High-risk systems are allowed to operate with only self-assessment in many cases, without mandatory third-party audits.  
   - Others claim the Act is too strict for low-risk applications, imposing red tape that stifles innovation, especially for startups and SMEs.  
   - *Source:* EDRi’s analysis (2023) called the Act “a missed opportunity” for robust oversight.

3. **General-Purpose AI (GPAI) and Foundation Models**  
   - The regulation of foundation models (e.g., GPT, Llama) was one of the most contentious issues. The final compromise exempts open-source models from many obligations, which critics say creates a loophole for powerful systems.  
   - Big tech companies (Google, Meta, OpenAI) lobbied heavily to weaken transparency and safety requirements for GPAI. The result is a tiered system with lighter rules for most models, only stricter for “systemic risk” models.  
   - *Source:* Politico reported in December 2023 that the GPAI provisions were “watered down” after intense lobbying.

4. **Competitiveness vs. Regulation – The Innovation Debate**  
   - European tech leaders and some member states (e.g., France, Germany) warned that the Act would hamper Europe’s ability to compete with the US and China. They argued that excessive regulation would drive AI development elsewhere.  
   - In contrast, proponents say the Act creates a single market with clear rules, which could actually boost trust and adoption.  
   - *Source:* A letter from 150+ European companies (2023) claimed the Act would “stifle innovation” and called for a more innovation-friendly approach.

5. **Lobbying and Big Tech Influence**  
   - Critics point to the heavy influence of corporate lobbyists during the legislative process. More than 1,000 lobbyists from big tech firms were registered, and many provisions were softened.  
   - For example, the original proposal for mandatory third-party conformity assessments for high-risk systems was replaced with self-assessment in most cases.  
   - *Source:* Corporate Europe Observatory (2023) documented the lobbying efforts.

6. **Exemptions for Law Enforcement and Biometric Surveillance**  
   - The Act bans real-time biometric surveillance in public spaces, but with broad exceptions for national security, crime prevention, and terrorism. Human rights groups say these exceptions gut the ban.  
   - The European Parliament had initially pushed for a total ban, but the final text allows member states to authorize such systems under judicial oversight.  
   - *Source:* Amnesty International and Access Now condemned the exemptions as a “green light for mass surveillance.”

7. **Open-Source Exemptions – A Double-Edged Sword**  
   - The Act exempts open-source AI models from most obligations unless they are placed on the market as a product or service. Critics argue this creates a gap where dangerous models can be freely distributed.  
   - Open-source advocates, however, say the exemption is necessary to avoid stifling research and community development.  
   - *Source:* The AI Act’s recitals explicitly exempt open-source components, a move praised by some but criticized by safety researchers.

8. **Bureaucracy and Compliance Costs**  
   - SMEs and startups worry about the cost of compliance, especially for high-risk systems. The Act requires documentation, risk management, human oversight, and transparency – which can be expensive.  
   - The European Commission has promised support (e.g., regulatory sandboxes, guidance), but critics say the burden is still disproportionate.  
   - *Source:* A study by the European SME United (2024) estimated compliance costs could reach €400,000 for a small company.

9. **Enforcement Fragmentation**  
   - The Act relies on national authorities (each member state designates a “notified body”) to enforce rules. Critics fear inconsistent application across the EU, leading to a patchwork of enforcement.  
   - The European AI Office (within the Commission) will oversee GPAI, but its powers are limited.  
   - *Source:* The AI Act’s governance structure has been called “complex and under-resourced” by legal experts.

10. **Human Rights and Civil Liberties Gaps**  
    - Many human rights organizations argue the Act does not go far enough to protect against algorithmic discrimination, bias, and social scoring.  
    - The Act bans “social scoring” by governments, but private-sector scoring (e.g., credit scoring) is not covered.  
    - *Source:* The UN Special Rapporteur on the right to privacy (2024) expressed concern that the Act lacks a strong human rights framework.

**Conclusion**  
The EU AI Act is a landmark piece of legislation, but it is deeply contested. Critics from both sides – those who want stronger regulation and those who want lighter touch – have raised valid concerns. The final text is a political compromise that leaves many issues unresolved. Key flashpoints include the definition of AI, GPAI regulation, law enforcement exemptions, and the balance between innovation and safety. As implementation begins in 2025, these controversies are likely to intensify, especially as the first enforcement actions and court challenges emerge.

| **CE Marking & Conformity Assessment** | **Two pathways**: <br>• **Self‑assessment** (if the provider can demonstrate compliance with all harmonised standards). <br>• **Third‑party assessment** (EU‑accredited notified bodies) for systems where the standards are not yet fully harmonised. | ‑ CE‑mark label on product, conformity‑assessment report (ATT), signed declaration of conformity. |

### 2.1. Harmonised Standards & Reference Specifications  

The EU has commissioned a set of **EN/ISO/IEC standards** that act as “technical specifications” for conformity assessment:

| Standard | Focus | Status (2024) |
|----------|-------|---------------|
| **EN ISO/IEC 22989** | Terminology & concepts for trustworthy AI. | Published 2023, used for documentation. |
| **EN ISO/IEC 23053** | AI system life‑cycle processes (risk, data, monitoring). | Draft, EU‑referenced. |
| **EN 716-1/2** | Specific to biometric facial‑recognition (accuracy, bias). | Mandatory for public‑space surveillance. |
| **CEN/TS 44277** | Transparency & explainability guidelines for AI. | Technical Specification, voluntary but heavily referenced. |

Providers can **declare conformity** by aligning their internal processes with these standards; a **Notified Body** can audit the alignment when the standard is not yet a full European Standard (EN).

## 3. The **Conformity‑Assessment Workflow** (Step‑by‑Step)

1. **Classification** – Provider uses the **AI‑Risk‑Classification Tool** (hosted by the European AI Office) to determine whether the system falls into a high‑risk category. The tool interrogates a taxonomy (e.g., “AI system used for remote biometric identification in public spaces” → high‑risk).  
2. **Technical Dossier Preparation** – All artefacts listed in the TD (model cards, data‑sheets, risk logs, test reports) are compiled in a **machine‑readable package** (XML/JSON) and stored in a **Secure EU‑Hosted Repository** (the *AI Registry*).  
3. **Self‑Assessment or Notified‑Body Review** –  
   *If self‑assessment:* provider signs a **Declaration of Conformity (DoC)** and uploads the dossier to the AI Registry.  
   *If third‑party:* a **Notified Body** conducts an on‑site audit, validates the RMS, runs independent robustness tests, and issues a **Certificate of Conformity**.  
4. **CE Marking** – After a successful assessment, the CE mark (including the AI‑specific “AI” logo) is affixed to the product/package.  
5. **Market Surveillance** – National Market Surveillance Authorities (MSAs) can **request the dossier**, perform spot‑checks, or issue corrective measures. Non‑compliance may lead to *financial penalties* up to **6 % of annual turnover** (or €30 M, whichever is higher).  
6. **Post‑Market Monitoring** – Providers must **continuously feed performance data** back to the AI Registry (via an API). If an incident triggers a risk‑threshold breach, the provider must **issue a corrective action notice** within **15 days** and, if needed, suspend the CE mark.

## 5. Who Must Do What? (Roles & Technical Duties)

| Role | Main Technical Duty | How it is Verified |
|------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Provider** (developer/manufacturer) | Build the AI system, compile complete technical documentation, run risk‑management & testing, ensure continuous monitoring. | Self‑declaration or notified‑body audit; artefacts uploaded to AI Registry. |
| **Distributor** (importer, reseller) | Verify that the CE‑marked product comes with a valid DoC and that the dossier is present in the Registry. | Spot‑check against Registry entries; can be fined for selling non‑conforming AI. |
| **User / Operator** (e.g., hospital, public authority) | Implement **human‑oversight procedures**, keep logs of AI‑driven decisions, report incidents. | Audits by MSAs; must retain logs for 5 years in a tamper‑evident format (e.g., blockchain‑anchored hash). |
| **Notified Body** | Conduct technical conformity audits, run independent robustness/accuracy tests, issue certificates. | Accredited under EU Regulation 765/2008; must follow ISO/IEC 17021‑1 procedures. |

## 7. Key Take‑aways (Technical Lens)

* **Risk‑based, not blanket** – Only systems that meet the statutory definition of “high‑risk” undergo the heavy technical compliance burden (RMS, data‑governance, post‑market monitoring).  
* **Conformity ≈ Documentation + Testing** – The AI Act turns legal compliance into a **software‑engineer‑friendly artefact set**: version‑controlled model cards, machine‑readable risk logs, and automated test‑suites that can be integrated in CI/CD pipelines.  
* **CE Mark is now AI‑aware** – The familiar **CE‑mark** now includes a specific **AI logo** and a mandatory link to the system’s entry in the **EU AI Registry**.  
* **Automation is encouraged** – The EU provides open‑source toolkits and APIs so that compliance can be **programmatically validated**, reducing the need for manual paperwork.  
* **Enforcement is data‑driven** – Market‑surveillance authorities can query the Registry in real time, compare logged performance metrics against the provider’s baseline, and trigger swift corrective actions.
